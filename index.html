Grace Coddington: Vogue 100 Festival

Fashion's rockstar stylist speaks to Lucinda Chambers about fainting at shows, her sketches and her inability to compromise.

Save for later

Latest News Industry Intel Lifestyle Events Intern Blogs Personal Space

Red hair ablaze, Grace Coddington, Creative Director of US Vogue, takes to the stage for the first talk of the Vogue 100 Festival. It’s just 10am, but the theatre is sold out, people standing where no seats are available.

Lucinda Chambers, Fashion Director of British Vogue and Coddington's old assistant, introduces her as a living legend and fashion’s rock star, and Grace scoffs, “you’ve always exaggerated and you still do!” She is still bemused by the fame that has befallen her ever since The September Issue, the documentary film of US Vogue from which she emerged a hero.

"I hate that thing!" She says, " The movie camera would always get in the way, everyone would stare at it. Even going through clothes rails, peoples’ reactions were different – mine were.”

(Grace Coddington visits Versailles in The September Issue)

Coddington has, after all, worked with world-renowned photographers and models to create Vogue's most fantastical and elaborate scenes for over 50 years; Alice in Wonderland, shot by Annie Leibovitz, staring Natalia Vodianova and a host of designers is arguably her most famous.

“Is the sudden attention bad, then?” Asks Lucinda. "Not at all,” Grace replies, “It cheers my day when somebody stops me on the street and says, ‘I love you’re photographs.’"

“I can’t multitask, I’m getting old.”

Grace Coddington

Whatever may have happened since The September Issue, (two books and a fragrance and an army of fans, to be precise), for Grace, it's a still about one thing: the photograph. “I’m very annoying and picky and I don’t like compromises,” she says, “I can’t multitask, I’m getting old.” Lucinda asks if she’s learning. “No! I’m getting old,” Grace insists with characteristic dry humour.

(An example of Coddington's drawings from her autobiography)

There's a lot you can learn from that uncompromising mind-set. Grace is the sovreign fashion editor, but she is stubborn and frank, it couldn’t be clearer. Her need for control comes from an emotional place – she has burst into tears at a Comme des Garcons show, and fainted at an Yves Saint Laurent show.

“Clothes speak to me,” she says, “I’m the last remaining person that sketches at shows. When people take pictures and videos and they don’t see the clothes. I draw everything I like. I’m not that fast at making up my mind, so I draw it in case I want to reference it later.”

Her fantasies are inspired by everything, “It might be Matisse, or someone I ran into on the Subway. When you mix them altogether it becomes yours. Certainly everything you see is a reference, and using references to Mattise or Georgia O’Keeffe makes the picture richer. I love just clothes, but I like when something else happens.”

“Solitude stood me in good stead for this job.”

Grace Coddington

Having grown up in a very remote part of Anglesey in Wales, Grace lived a solitary childhood. Her parents owned a hotel and her sister left to marry young. She would play with her bike and sail in the bay on her little boat, cut off from the world and completely immersed in her imagination. “I think the solitude stood me in good stead for this job,” she says, “It probably made me a bit slow. I used to sit and dream a lot and I just had to come home in time for dinner. You’re fed this beautiful nature in your head and all around you, all day.”

It's a romantic image, the young red-head sailing in her boat 'till dinner time. The biggest thing you can learn from Grace Coddington is to follow your instinct and believe in it ruthlessly. When you feel something special is about to happen, don't let it go for anything.
